Research Guide

Genealogy & Local History Research

Resources, tools, and guidance for tracing your family history and exploring the story of Opelika and Lee County.

Where do I begin?

Genealogy research can feel overwhelming at first, but starting simple is the right instinct. Before diving into databases, take a few minutes to write down what you already know: full names, approximate birth years, places of residence, and any family stories you've heard. The answers you already have will point you toward the questions worth asking next.

A good first question is always: what is the earliest generation I can document, and where did they live? Working backward from the present, one generation at a time, is almost always more productive than trying to leap directly to distant ancestors.

What to bring to your research visit

  • Names, dates, and places you already know — even approximate ones are useful
  • Any documents you have at home: birth certificates, obituaries, old photographs, letters, family bibles
  • A notebook or laptop for recording what you find and where you found it
  • Your library card (required for access to some databases)

A note on archival materials

If your research leads you to original archival materials — manuscripts, photographs, historic documents — a few simple guidelines protect both you and the collection:

  • Use pencil only at the research table (no pens or markers)
  • Handle photographs and fragile items with gloves, which the archivist will provide
  • No food or beverages in the reading room
  • Personal photography is welcome; flash is not

The archivist retrieves all materials — you won't need to navigate the stacks yourself. Just ask.

Not sure where to start? Ask us.

The archivist is here to help you find what you're looking for — or figure out what to look for next. Don't hesitate to ask for a consultation, whether you're a first-time researcher or returning with a stubborn brick wall. Walk-ins are welcome during research hours, or contact us in advance to schedule an appointment.

Eight practical strategies for getting the most out of your genealogical research — whether you're just starting out or working through a persistent brick wall.

1

Cast a wide net on spelling

Names were recorded by ear, often by clerks who had never met your ancestor. The same person might appear as "Tompkins," "Thompkins," and "Tomkins" across different documents. When searching databases, try alternate spellings and use wildcard characters (usually an asterisk: Thomp*) when the platform allows it.

2

Every source has a source

When you find information in a published genealogy, a family tree on Ancestry, or a county history book, treat it as a lead, not a conclusion. Follow it back to the original record. The further a piece of information is from the original document, the more opportunities there have been for errors to creep in.

3

Document everything — including dead ends

Record not just what you find, but where you looked and came up empty. A note that says "searched Lee County deed books 1870–1890, no results for John Mercer" saves you from repeating that search six months from now and prevents you from chasing the same dead end twice.

4

Primary sources vs. secondary sources

A primary source was created at or near the time of the event by someone with direct knowledge — a birth certificate, a marriage license, a census record. A secondary source was created later, often from memory or other records — an obituary, a death certificate, a published family history. Both are useful; neither is infallible. Even primary sources contain errors.

5

The census is a starting point, not an endpoint

Federal census records (available through 1950) are often a researcher's first stop, and for good reason — they can place a family in a specific location at a specific time. But ages are frequently approximated, names are misspelled, and birthplaces are vague. Use the census to orient yourself, then look for records that can confirm or correct what it tells you.

6

Work with what the record was created to do

Every record was created for a specific administrative purpose, not for your research. A death certificate was created to document a death — the informant may not have known the deceased's exact birthdate or parents' names. Understanding why a record was created helps you evaluate how reliable it's likely to be for any given piece of information.

7

When you hit a brick wall, change the question

If you can't find a person, try finding their neighbors, siblings, or community. People in the same county often appear in each other's records — as witnesses on deeds, bondsmen on marriage licenses, or fellow church members. Cluster research — studying the people around your ancestor — frequently breaks open cases that direct searches cannot.

8

Cite as you go

It is much easier to record where you found something when you find it than to reconstruct that information later. A minimal citation — database name, collection title, record type, and the date you accessed it — is enough to let you or someone else find the record again.

Free Resources

Free

FamilySearch

familysearch.org

The largest free genealogy database in the world, maintained by the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Includes billions of digitized records — census, vital records, military, probate, and more — spanning hundreds of countries and centuries. An excellent first stop for any research project.

Free

Chronicling America

chroniclingamerica.loc.gov

A searchable database of historic American newspapers from 1770–1963, developed by the Library of Congress and NEH. Includes several Alabama newspapers. Obituaries, marriage announcements, and local news items can break open genealogical brick walls.

Free

Find A Grave

findagrave.com

A crowdsourced database of cemetery records and memorial pages, with photographs of headstones from cemeteries worldwide. Particularly useful for confirming death dates, locating burial sites, and identifying family clusters. Coverage of rural Alabama cemeteries is substantial and growing.

Free

BillionGraves

billiongraves.com

A GPS-tagged headstone photograph database similar to Find A Grave, with an emphasis on indexed, searchable records. The two databases have different coverage — searching both is worth the effort.

Free

USGenWeb Project

usgenweb.org

A volunteer-driven network of free genealogical resources organized by state and county. The Alabama GenWeb project links to transcribed records, local histories, and researcher resources organized by county, including Lee County.

Free

Freedmen's Bureau Records

familysearch.org/en/freedmensbureau

Digitized and indexed records from the Bureau of Refugees, Freedmen, and Abandoned Lands (1865–1872). An essential resource for African American genealogy research in the post-Civil War South. Records include labor contracts, marriage registers, and ration records.

Subscription Resources

The following databases require a paid subscription. Alabama residents may be able to access some of these through the Alabama Virtual Library (AVL) — check avl.lib.al.us or ask a librarian. Some databases may also be available on library computers.

Subscription

Ancestry

ancestry.com

The largest subscription genealogy platform, with extensive U.S. and international records including census, vital records, military, immigration, and newspapers. Also hosts user-submitted family trees — treat these as leads rather than verified sources.

Subscription

Newspapers.com

newspapers.com

A large archive of digitized historical newspapers, including many Alabama titles. Particularly strong for obituaries, marriage and birth announcements, and local news. Owned by Ancestry; some access is included with Ancestry subscriptions.

Subscription

Fold3

fold3.com

Specializes in military records — pension files, draft registrations, service records, and unit histories from the Revolutionary War through the twentieth century. An essential resource for any research involving military service.

Subscription

MyHeritage

myheritage.com

A genealogy platform with strong international holdings, particularly useful for European research. Also offers DNA testing services and a photo enhancement tool that can help identify individuals in old photographs.

Subscription

FindMyPast

findmypast.com

Particularly strong in British Isles records, but also holds U.S. collections including historical newspapers and census records. Useful for researchers with Irish, English, Scottish, or Welsh ancestry.

Don't see what you need? Ask the archivist — access to genealogical records is constantly expanding, and there may be resources available that aren't listed here. Contact mrice@opelika-al.gov with questions.
